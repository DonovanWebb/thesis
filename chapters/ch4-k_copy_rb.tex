Revealing Correlated Errors through Symmetry in Parallel Randomised Benchmarking
% Abstract
Structured noise in quantum processors can invalidate the independence assumptions underlying
many noise models and error-correction protocols, and as such constitutes a critical process to characterise in large-scale fault-tolerant quantum computation. Explicitly accounting for
such correlations typically requires characterisation techniques that scale
poorly with system size.
We present a scalable method for quantifying correlated noise using parallel
(``k-copy") Clifford randomised benchmarking. By applying identical Clifford sequences
across multiple qubits, the protocol enforces a symmetry that decomposes the
dynamics into invariant subspaces, enabling direct separation of correlated
and uncorrelated error components from two-qubit measurement outcomes.
We experimentally demonstrate this method on a trapped-ion quantum
processor, resolving two-body correlations across up to 8 ions. Parity
measurements further reveal the underlying symmetry-resolved dynamics, with
correlated noise driving population into invariant subspaces that can be
observed without full process reconstruction.
This approach provides a practical tool for diagnosing structured noise in
quantum devices and for informing the design of device-specific error
mitigation and error-correction strategies.
%
% ===== Punchy Intro
% Why correlated noise matters:
As quantum processors scale, fault-tolerance, and error mitigation become necessary to
overcome decoherence and systematic control errors~\cite{shor_fault-tolerant_1996, aharonov_fault-tolerant_2008, cai_quantum_2023}. Crucially, errors
which induce correlations across multiple qubits require mitigation and correction strategies built on noise
models that faithfully represent their correlated structure; assuming
simple white noise models can lead to systematic underestimation of logical error rates~\cite{aharonov_fault-tolerant_2006}, or systematic bias of the corrected observable outcome~\cite{foldager_can_2024}.
It is often assumed that localised gate errors are scrambled throughout complex circuits to more amenable depolarisation, or white noise channels, as was shown in the case of sufficiently deep random circuits~\cite{dalzell_random_2024, arute_quantum_2019}. However, for near-term shallow circuits, and highly structured deep circuits, this assumption does not hold~\cite{foldager_can_2024}. 
% Previous work
Noise channels can be coerced to more favourable models via twirling or random circuit compilation~\cite{geller_efficient_2013, wallman_noise_2016, hashim_benchmarking_2023}, however, these methods must be optimised and verified on device.
Determining an appropriate noise model in a given device therefore demands
characterization protocols that directly diagnose correlated noise, ideally with minimal experimental overhead. 
Many such
characterization methods exist, based on gate and process tomography~\cite{rudinger_experimental_2021},
the study of collective spin phenomena~\cite{gulacsi_characterizing_2025},
noise learning~\cite{harper_efficient_2020, harper_learning_2023, nielsen_efficient_2021}, random circuits~\cite{sarovar_detecting_2020}, and several randomised benchmarking (RB) techniques
~\cite{mckay_correlated_2020, erhard_characterizing_2019, gambetta_characterization_2012}. 
% Why do we need this new technique?
These methods vary drastically between high expressibility of the measured error channel (e.g. tomographic methods), to high scalability of the protocol (e.g. RB methods), but often must compromise between these two aspects. We suggest a scalable RB method, named~\emph{k-copy RB}, that retains the ability to discern the quality of correlated noise, e.g. if the origin is due to fluctuating global fields, an entangling channel, or correlated non-unital errors. 
% Highlight difference to Correlated RB
A related protocol, termed ``Correlated RB''~\cite{mckay_correlated_2020} (based on ``Simultaneous RB''~\cite{gambetta_characterization_2012}), uses independent local twirling of multiple system copies to estimate a distance of the measured channel to a product channel (non-correlated). In contrast, ``k-copy RB'' uses identical local twirling to preserve symmetry sectors, so spatially correlated noise appears as population trapped in, or leaking between, identifiable multi-qubit subspaces.
Previously, the technique of observing subspace decay rates has been used
to benchmark leakage errors during two-qubit
entanglement~\cite{sutherland_subspace_2025}, and in suppressing the detrimental
effects of laser decoherence in correlation spectroscopy
measurements~\cite{clements_lifetime-limited_2020}. \\

\indent\textit{Protocol\textemdash}Our method distinguishes correlated noise through parallel, ``k-copy", application of the single-qubit Clifford group, $Cl^{\otimes k}$~\cite{wallman_randomized_2014,helsen_representations_2018}. 
Here, the enforced symmetry preserves a set of two-qubit subspaces (tabulated in
supplemental~\ref{table:irreps}). These subspaces provide a natural basis to
study the action of the generic noise channel. For a subset of noise channels,
which we consider here, the subspaces may be considered independent of each
other. However, in general, a noise channel can couple subspaces together (if
they are isotypic representations). Through observing the dynamic populations of
these subspaces, e.g. measuring decay rates, the noise type and magnitude can be
determined. For two qubits, these noise channels can
be~\cite{sarovar_detecting_2020}: independent depolarising channels, common
single-qubit rotations, two-qubit entanglement, correlated non-unital, and
two-qubit swaps.\\
%
% Protocol
``k-copy" RB is executed experimentally by applying
identical single qubit Clifford RB sequences to all qubits, as seen for one
random sequence of length $M$ in figure~\ref{fig:1} (a). As with standard
Clifford benchmarking~\cite{dankert_exact_2009, magesan_scalable_2011}, $r$ random Clifford
sequences are generated with length $M$ to ensure that the ensemble of random
circuit approximates twirling over the group. To build statistics of the final
state populations, each sequence is repeated $s$ times. Classical post
processing is required for calculating pairwise correlated populations from
per-shot data. 
The correlated populations for a given pair $(i,j)$ are denoted
by $\mathcal{P}_{SS}^{(i,j)}, \mathcal{P}_{SF}^{(i,j)},
\mathcal{P}_{SS}^{(i,j)}, \mathcal{P}_{FF}^{(i,j)}$, with $S$ and $F$
representing success and failure of measuring the target outcome, respectively.
The target outcome is a computational eigenstate, which is randomised between $\ket{0}$ and $\ket{1}$ for
each generated sequence, to account for asymmetry in readout and state preparation~\cite{knill_randomized_2008}. \\
As an example of what ``k-copy" reveals, figure~\ref{fig:1}~(b) shows schematically the final state after only a 
dominant common rotation error affects qubits $i=3$ and $i=4$, while other qubits are dominated by independent noise. If the common rotations are probabilistic, or the channel is twirled over $Cl^{\otimes k}$, these common rotations lead to spatial correlations. These correlations are revealed through repeating the RB circuit for different sequences of the same total length, $M$. In figure~\ref{fig:1}~(c) $5$ final states before measurement are shown overlaid on the same Bloch spheres for qubits $i={1,2,3,4}$. The black arrows along $Z$ are the depolarised density matrix, $\rho_i$, inferred if standard RB is used. It can be seen that $\rho_i$ for all $4$ qubits are identical, suggesting each has the same magnitude single-qubit error per Clifford. However, if we compare qubits pairwise, we can see there is a symmetry between qubits dominated by correlated noise (highlighted by dashed line pairing sequences), while the uncorrelated case has none. This extra symmetry between qubit pairs allows us to break down the total error per Clifford into spatially correlated and uncorrelated components.
% Hook line:
These components are quantified by fitting the correlated populations, which are in general multiple exponential decays (if the Liouville form is diagonalisable). This is
similar to other non-standard RB techniques~\cite{sutherland_subspace_2025, helsen_new_2019}, where decay rates relate to populations trapped or slowly leaking from invariant subspaces.\\

% Analytic decay (general)
The analytic model considered here describes correlated random-unitary single-qubit noise~\cite{sarovar_detecting_2020}, here termed \emph{common noise}: each RB step applies a tensor product of single-qubit rotations whose random variables may be shared between qubits. This class captures correlated but non-entangling control and environmental fluctuations, which are particularly relevant
to trapped-ion and neutral atom qubits, exposed to common 
fluctuations of qubit drive phase, amplitude, and detuning; magnetic-field noise and
gradients; beam pointing and intensity drift; AC Stark shift fluctuations;
motional-state–dependent over-rotations; and gate duration calibration errors.
It does not describe entangling coherent errors, correlated non-unital processes, leakage, or general non-Markovian noise. A discussion on entangling coherent errors, is given in the supplemental.
With these assumptions, the probability of both qubits successfully returning to the initial state is given by, 
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:decay}
    \begin{split}
        \mathcal{P}_{SS} &=  \frac{1}{4}+\frac{1}{4}(\alpha_L^m+\alpha_R^m) + \frac{1}{6}\alpha_{dt}^m+\frac{1}{12}\alpha_{tr}^m,   \\
        % \mathcal{P}_{FF} &=  \frac{1}{4}-\frac{1}{4}(\alpha_L^m+\alpha_R^m) + \frac{1}{6}\alpha_{dt}^m+\frac{1}{12}\alpha_{tr}^m,   \\
        % \mathcal{P}_{SF} &=~  \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{4}(\alpha_L^m-\alpha_R^m) - \frac{1}{6}\alpha_{dt}^m-\frac{1}{12}\alpha_{tr}^m,   \\
        % \mathcal{P}_{FS} &=~  \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{4}(\alpha_L^m-\alpha_R^m) - \frac{1}{6}\alpha_{dt}^m-\frac{1}{12}\alpha_{tr}^m,   \\
        \end{split}
\end{equation}
where $m$ is the Clifford sequence length, and
$\alpha_i$ are the scalar decay rates for each sector corresponding to the sub-representations of the irreducible representations (irreps) of the 2-copy single qubit Clifford group~\cite{helsen_representations_2018}. $\alpha_L$ and $\alpha_R$ describe the decays of the local subspaces for the left and right qubit respectively, $\alpha_{dt}$ describes the decay for the ``diagonal traceless" $2-$dimensional irrep, and $\alpha_{tr}$ is the decay rate of the $1-$dimensional ``symmetric trace" subspace.
Similar relations for $\mathcal{P}_{SF}$, $\mathcal{P}_{FS}$, and $\mathcal{P}_{FF}$ are used for fitting experimental data, and given in the supplemental.
% Common noise error channel
These scalar decay rates are linked to experimentally relevant parameters by considering a collection of specific error channels. We consider independent depolarisation channels, parametrised by $\varepsilon_L, \varepsilon_R$, and, 
the physically motivated error model of \emph{common noise}.
%
This noise channel is given by,
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:rotation_channel}
    \rho\mapsto\mathcal{E}^A_{U\otimes U}(\rho)=\sum_a p_a(U_a\otimes U_a)\rho(U_a\otimes U_a)^\dagger,
\end{equation}
where the channel is an ensemble over distribution $A$ of unitaries applied with probability $p_a$ to the initial state, with $U_a(\theta_a,\hat{n}_a)=e^{-i(\theta_a/2)\hat n_a\cdot\hat\sigma}$, with rotation angle, $\theta_a$, axis, $\hat n_a$, and Pauli vector, $\hat\sigma$.
% Analytic decay (Common noise)
To first order the decays of each sector are then, 
\begin{align}\label{eqn:bases}
    \alpha_L &= 1-\varepsilon_L-\varepsilon_\mathrm{corr},& \alpha_R &= 1-\varepsilon_R-\varepsilon_\mathrm{corr},\\
    \alpha_{tr} &= 1 - \varepsilon_L- \varepsilon_R,& \alpha_{dt} &= 1 - \varepsilon_L- \varepsilon_R - 3\varepsilon_\mathrm{corr},
    % \alpha_{dt} &=\alpha_{so}= 1 - \varepsilon_L- \varepsilon_R - 3\varepsilon_{corr},\\
    % \alpha_{as} &=  1 - \varepsilon_L- \varepsilon_R - \varepsilon_{corr},\\
\end{align}
where the correlated error per Clifford, $\varepsilon_\mathrm{corr}$, quantifies \textit{common noise} with,
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon_\mathrm{corr}=\frac{1}{3}\sum_ap_a\theta_a^2.
\end{equation}
% Asymptotes
The trace subspace is seen to be invariant under \textit{common noise} leading to  
the asymptote $\mathcal{P}_{SS}\rightarrow 1/3$ for
$\varepsilon_L=\varepsilon_R = 0$, $\varepsilon_\mathrm{corr} > 0$. 
%${\mathcal{P}_{SS}, \mathcal{P}_{FF}, (\mathcal{P}_{SF}+\mathcal{P}_{FS})}\rightarrow 1/3$. If there is some finite local error rates for each qubit, then the asymptotes correspond to ${\mathcal{P}_{SS}, \mathcal{P}_{FF}, \frac{\mathcal{P}_{SF}+\mathcal{P}_{FS}}{2}}\rightarrow 1/4$.  
If there is some finite local error rates for each qubit, then the asymptote corresponds to $\mathcal{P}_{SS}\rightarrow 1/4$.  
In the realistic case that both errors are present, and correlated errors dominate over
independent errors, a clear intermediate asymptote corresponding to the
symmetric trace subspace can be seen in the correlated population decay curves, before a slow
decay into the fully depolarised two-qubit state.

The two-qubit mixed state, upon reaching the symmetric trace asymptote, is given by,
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:bell}
    \begin{split}
        \rho_{sym} &= \frac{1}{3}\bigl(\ket{00}\bra{00}+\ket{11}\bra{11}+ \ket{\Psi^+}\bra{\Psi^+}\bigr),\\
    \end{split}
\end{equation}
where $\ket{\Psi^+}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{01}+\ket{10})$ is a Bell state, and displays induced coherence between the two qubits. This coherence may be measured through parity oscillations of the two-qubit state after ``k-copy" twirling. We note that the parity contrast as a function of Clifford sequence length has dependency on sector decay terms,
\begin{equation}
\label{eqn:parity}
    C =\frac{1}{3}(\alpha_{tr}^m-\alpha_{dt}^m),
\end{equation}
which may be conveniently combined with correlated population measurements to isolate individual sector decay rates via fitting single exponential decays. A short discussion of which can be found in the supplemental.\\

\subsection{k-copy Clifford analysis}\label{app:theory}
We begin this section by considering the representations of the single-qubit Clifford group acting diagonally on two copies, $Cl^{\otimes2}$ (see ref.~\cite{helsen_representations_2018} for in detail analysis). The irreducible representations give a natural (Schur) basis to consider how possible error channels are simplified upon the ``k-copy" RB protocol. We finish by deriving the correlated populations given in equation~\ref{eqn:decay}, and observed experimentally when the system is subject to common random-unitary single-qubit noise.

\subsubsection{Representations of the 2-copy Clifford group}
Twirling over group $\mathcal{G}$ enforces symmetry on a general error channel, $\Lambda$, given in Liouville form,
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:twirl}
    \Lambda\mapsto\tilde\Lambda=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{G}|}\sum_\mathcal{G}G\Lambda G^\dagger,
\end{equation}
where $G$ are the vectorised representations of group elements of $\mathcal{G}$. The twirled channel now satisfies the commutative relation,
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:commute}
    G\tilde\Lambda G^\dagger=\tilde\Lambda~~~~\forall G \in \mathcal{G}.
\end{equation}
The representation of the space, $\mathcal{H}$ decomposes to irreps $\lambda$ as,
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{H}=\bigoplus_\lambda(V_\lambda\otimes \mathcal C^{m_\lambda}),
\end{equation}
where $V_\lambda$ is the irreducible representation space, and $\mathcal{C}^{m_\lambda}$ is the multiplicity space with dimension of the multiplicity $m_\lambda$.
From Shur's lemma, equation~\ref{eqn:commute} implies the commuting twirled error channel has the form,
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:block-diag}
    \tilde\Lambda=\bigoplus_\lambda(I_\lambda\otimes \mathcal A_{\lambda}),
\end{equation}
where $A_\lambda$ is the multiplicity matrix of dimensions $m_\lambda\times m_\lambda.$\\
The 2-copy single-qubit Clifford group, analysed in detail in references~\cite{helsen_representations_2018, wallman_randomized_2014}, decomposes
the general $16$-dimensional two qubit space into 4 irreducible representations, irreps, with dimensions $\{1,2,3,3'\}$, (the notation $3'$ is used to contrast $3$), and multiplicities $\{2,1,3,1\}$. The sub-representations are summarised in table~\ref{table:irreps}, and the character table is given in~\ref{table:character}.

These field fluctuations induce both common and independent single-qubit rotations, but cannot lead to any couplings $\{m\}$.

The structure of two random single-qubit rotation noise channel generally acts independently on each sub-representation, and so is parametrised by at most the $6$ scalars, $\{\alpha\}$.\\
As such, the Liouville form in the Schur basis (ordered by table~\ref{table:irreps}) of the general error map without multiplicity coupling is given by diagonal matrix,
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:Liouville}
    \tilde\Lambda = diag^\mathcal{S}\Bigl(I_1, \alpha_{L} I_3, \alpha_{R} I_3, \alpha_{tr} I_1, \alpha_{dt} I_2, \alpha_{so} I_3, \alpha_{as} I_3\Bigr),
\end{equation}
where $diag^\mathcal{S}\left(\cdot\right)$ refers to a block diagonal transfer matrix given in the Schur, $\mathcal{S}$, basis.
Let us consider the effect of noise due to common single-qubit rotations acting on two-qubits, with error channels of a general form,
\begin{equation}
    \rho\mapsto\mathcal{E}^{(M)}_{U\otimes U}(\rho)=\sum_m p_m(U_m\otimes U_m)\rho(U_m\otimes U_m)^\dagger,
\end{equation}
where the sum is over a distribution, $M$, of single qubit unitaries each applied with probability $p_m$, with $\sum_m p_m=1$ to satisfy trace preservation.
In general, this error channel cannot be described by the separable map,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{E}(\rho_1\otimes\rho_2) = \mathcal{E}_1(\rho_1)\otimes\mathcal{E}_2(\rho_2),
\end{equation}
where $\rho_i$ is the density matrix of qubit $i$, as such, the channel generally induces spatial correlations.\\
The single qubit rotation is parametrised by rotation angle, $\theta$, and the axis of rotation $\vec{n}$. 
\begin{equation}
U(\theta,\hat{n})=e^{-i\frac{\theta}{2}\hat n\cdot\hat\sigma }.
\end{equation}
We now consider the channel twirled over a discrete group $\mathcal{G}$ with elements $G$, with twirling defined by equation~\ref{eqn:twirl}.
Considering the form of equation~\ref{eqn:rotation_channel}, the twirled channel may be written as,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{\tilde E}=\sum_m p_m \mathcal{\tilde E}_m,
\end{equation}
where 
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{\tilde E}_m(\rho)=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{G}|}\sum_\mathcal{G} G(U\otimes U)\rho(U\otimes U)^\dagger G^\dagger,
\end{equation}
is the channel for diagonally applied coherent unitary rotations.\\
The goal is now to find the scalar parameters $\{\alpha\}$, which describe this channel.
It can be seen from the basis vectors, that only
the “Trace” sector is preserved under global single-qubit
rotations, $\alpha_{tr}=1$. It is in fact this preservation that leads to the decay into the symmetric state given in the main text equation~\ref{eqn:bell}.
The other scalar parameters can be calculated ``mechanically" by constructing the Pauli-transfer matrix (PTM) representation and twirling over $Cl^{\otimes 2}$, however, we note that the implicit symmetry of each sub-representation can be used to define tensor contractions acting on the $SO(3)^{\otimes 2}$ space spanned by two rotations,
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        s_{tr} &= \sum_{i,j,k,l}\delta_{ij}\delta_{kl}A_{ki}B_{ij}=Tr(A^TB),\\
        s_{id} &= \sum_{i,j,k,l}\delta_{ik}\delta_{jl}A_{ki}B_{ij}=Tr(A)Tr(B),\\
        s_{swap} &= \sum_{i,j,k,l}\delta_{il}\delta_{jk}A_{ki}B_{ij}=Tr(AB),\\
        s_{diag} &= \sum_{i,j,k,l}(\delta_{ik}\delta_{jl})\delta_{ij}A_{ki}B_{ij}=\sum_i (A_{ii}B_{ii}),\\
    \end{split}
\end{equation}
where $A$ and $B$ are the $SO(3)$ representation of independent unitaries acting on the left and right qubits respectively.
The scalar parameters $\{\alpha\}$ are then found by,
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        \alpha_{tr} &= \frac{1}{3}s_{tr},\\
        \alpha_{dt} &= \frac{1}{2}s_{diag}- \frac{1}{6}s_{tr},\\
        \alpha_{so} &= \frac{1}{6}(s_{id}+s_{swap})-\frac{1}{3}s_{diag},\\
        \alpha_{as} &= \frac{1}{6}(s_{id}-s_{swap}).\\
    \end{split}
\end{equation}
In the case of a global rotation, $A=B$, we find,
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        \alpha_{L}&=\alpha_R= \alpha_{as} =\frac{1+2\cos\theta}{3},\\
        \alpha_{tr} &= 1,\\
        \alpha_{dt} &= \frac{1}{2} \Bigl(1 + \cos(2 \theta) + 8 n_{an} \sin(\theta/2)^4\Bigr),\\
        \alpha_{so} &= \frac{1}{3}  \Bigl(2 \cos(\theta) + \cos(2 \theta) - 
   8  n_{an}\sin(\theta/2)^4\Bigr),\\\\
    \end{split}
\end{equation}
with an anisotropic term, $n_{an}=n_y^4 + n_z^4 + n_y^2n_z^2 - n_y^2-n_z^2$.
Taking these scalar products to second order in error angle $\theta$,
we find that the anisotropic term may be ignored for small errors,
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        \alpha_L&=\alpha_R=\alpha_{as}= 1- \theta^2/3, \\
        \alpha_{tr} &= 1,\\
        \alpha_{dt} &= \alpha_{so} = 1- \theta^2. \\
    \end{split}
\end{equation}
As such, the dynamics are entirely described by the error angle magnitude.
The ensemble of twirled common unitaries across some arbitrary distribution, $M$, given in equation~\ref{eqn:rotation_channel}, thus 
combine to give scalar parameters describing the contribution from each subspace to the observed decay,
\begin{equation}
\alpha^{(M)}_i = \sum_m p_m \alpha_i^{(m)}.
\end{equation}
We further define the correlated error per Clifford, 
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon_{corr}=\frac{1}{3}\sum_mp_m\theta_m^2,
\end{equation}
to conform with other RB work.
Comparing to equation~\ref{eqn:Liouville}, the general ensemble of common single-qubit rotations channel, after twirling over $Cl^{\otimes 2}$ is given by the diagonal matrix in the Schur basis,
\begin{equation}
    \tilde\Lambda_{U\otimes U} = diag^\mathcal{S}\Bigl(I_1, (1-\varepsilon_{corr}) I_6,  I_1, (1-3\varepsilon_{corr}) I_2, (1-\varepsilon_{corr}) I_6).
\end{equation}
Ultimately, environmental noise will not perfectly couple symmetrically to both qubits, and so independent local single-qubit error channels must also be considered.
Assuming an independent noise channel acting on each qubit, Clifford twirling leads to a fully depolarising noise channel of the form,
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:1q_depol}
    \rho^{(1q)}\mapsto\mathcal{E}_{dp,a}(\rho^{(1q)})=(1-\varepsilon_a)\rho^{(1q)}+ \varepsilon_a\frac{I}{2}Tr(\rho^{(1q)}),
\end{equation}
where the superscript denotes that this is for a single qubit subspace, and the subscript $a$ signifies which qubit we are considering. For the ``left" and ``right" qubits the decay rates are parametrised by $\varepsilon_L$ and $\varepsilon_R$, respectively.
Comparing to equation~\ref{eqn:Liouville}, the independent depolarisation channel may be expressed in Liouville form as the diagonal matrix in the Pauli basis,
\begin{equation}
    \Lambda_{dp} = diag^\mathcal{P}\Bigl(1,(1-\varepsilon_L)I_3,(1-\varepsilon_R)I_3,(1-\varepsilon_L)(1-\varepsilon_R)I_9\Bigr),
\end{equation}
where $diag^\mathcal{P}\left(\cdot\right)$ represents the block diagonal matrix in the ordered two-qubit Pauli basis $\mathcal{P}=\{I\otimes I, \vec\sigma\otimes I , I\otimes\vec\sigma, \vec\sigma\otimes\vec\sigma\}$, with $\vec\sigma = \{X, Y, Z\}$.
Combining the depolarisation channels with the twirled common single-qubit rotation channel, 
\begin{equation}
    \Lambda_{T}(\varepsilon_L, \varepsilon_R,\varepsilon_{corr})=\Lambda_{dp}(\varepsilon_L,\varepsilon_R)\tilde\Lambda_{corr}(\varepsilon_{corr}),
\end{equation}
we find scalar parameters,
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:app_bases}
    \begin{split}
        \alpha_L &= 1-\varepsilon_L-\varepsilon_{corr},\\
        \alpha_R &= 1-\varepsilon_R-\varepsilon_{corr},\\
        \alpha_{tr} &= 1 - \varepsilon_L- \varepsilon_R,\\
        \alpha_{dt} &=\alpha_{so}= 1 - \varepsilon_L- \varepsilon_R - 3\varepsilon_{corr},\\
        \alpha_{as} &=  1 - \varepsilon_L- \varepsilon_R - \varepsilon_{corr},\\
    \end{split}
\end{equation}
which fully describe the error channels we consider in the main text.\\
During the proposed RB experiment, the initial state is always prepared to be $\ket{0,0}$, and the final correlated populations correspond to projecting onto the states $\{\ket{0,0}, \ket{0,1}, \ket{1,0}, \ket{1,1}\}$. In the main text, we consider ``success" and ``failure" rather than ``0", and ``1", as we allow for target state randomisation, however this detail is excluded here, and we assume that the noise-less RB sequence results in the identity operation.
The correlated populations may be given analytically using the derived Liouville operators, and by the vectorized initial and final states,
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:rb_meas}
    \mathcal{P}_{L,R}=\langle\langle L,R|\Lambda_T^m|00\rangle\rangle,
\end{equation}
where $L,R$ denote the final projected computational state for the ``left" and ``right" qubit (either $\ket{0}$ or $\ket{1}$) respectively, and $m$ is the number of Cliffords in the applied sequence.
Using this equation, and the explicit transfer matrices, the final populations decays are found,
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:app_pops}
    \begin{split}
        \mathcal{P}_{0,0} &=  \frac{1}{4}+\frac{1}{4}(\alpha_L^m+\alpha_R^m) + \frac{1}{6}\alpha_{dt}^m+\frac{1}{12}\alpha_{tr}^m,   \\
        \mathcal{P}_{0,1} &=  \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{4}(\alpha_L^m-\alpha_R^m) - \frac{1}{6}\alpha_{dt}^m-\frac{1}{12}\alpha_{tr}^m,   \\
        \mathcal{P}_{1,0} &=  \frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{4}(\alpha_L^m-\alpha_R^m) - \frac{1}{6}\alpha_{dt}^m-\frac{1}{12}\alpha_{tr}^m,   \\
        \mathcal{P}_{1,1} &=  \frac{1}{4}-\frac{1}{4}(\alpha_L^m+\alpha_R^m) + \frac{1}{6}\alpha_{dt}^m+\frac{1}{12}\alpha_{tr}^m.   \\
        \end{split}
\end{equation}
Considering the final state of a single qubit after ``k-copy" RB, we find the expected single exponential decay,
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:app_1qdecay}
    \mathcal{P}_0^{(L)}=\mathcal{P}_{0,0}+\mathcal{P}_{0,1}= \frac{1}{2}(1 + (1-\varepsilon_L-\varepsilon_{corr})^m),
\end{equation}
where the typical ``error-per-Clifford" parameter, $\varepsilon_c$, in standard Clifford RB is here separated into a correlated and independent error component, 
\begin{equation}
    \varepsilon_c^{(L)}=\varepsilon_L+\varepsilon_{corr},
\end{equation}
which through fitting the correlated populations may be quantified.\\
Standard Clifford RB also reveals a state-preparation and measurement, ``SPAM", error as an initial scaling factor of the exponential decay. The effects of SPAM are discussed in section~\ref{app:spam}.

\end{equation}

\end{split}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Parity analysis}\label{app:parity}
Figure~\ref{fig:2}~(b) in the main text shows the contrast of parity measurements after a differential $\frac{\pi}{2}-$pulse is applied to the two qubits. This measurement directly probes coherences $\ket{01}\bra{10}$ and $\ket{10}\bra{01}$. The analytic model of the parity contrast (black dashed line in figure), can be simply calculated by extending equation~\ref{eqn:rb_meas}, to include a final differential rotation, which we refer to as the parity pulse, in transfer matrix form, $\Lambda_\Pi$,
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:app_parcontrast}
\begin{split}
    \Pi(\phi)&=\langle\langle \pi|\Lambda_\Pi(\phi)\Lambda_T^m|00\rangle\rangle,\\
    &= C\cos(2\phi),
\end{split}
\end{equation}
where $\Pi$ is the parity signal, $|\pi\rangle\rangle=|00\rangle\rangle+|11\rangle\rangle-|01\rangle\rangle-|10\rangle\rangle$, $\phi$ describes the rotation axis of the differential pulse, and $C$ is the parity contrast.
The differential parity pulses are parallel single qubit rotations $\hat{R}^{(L)}_{+\phi}(\pi/2)$ applied to the left qubit, and $\hat{R}^{(R)}_{-\phi}(\pi/2)$ to the right qubit, with rotation axis$\sigma_\phi = \cos(\phi)\sigma_x+\sin(\phi)\sigma_y$. 
The parity contrast, which effectively measures the overlap between the final mixed state after RB, and the pure Bell state $\ket{\Psi^+}$, (this is a typical reduced tomography method, see ref.~\cite{sackett_experimental_2000}), has the form,
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:parity_contrast}
    C=\frac{1}{3}(\alpha_{tr}^m-\alpha_{dt}^m)
\end{equation}
with $\alpha_{tr}$ and $\alpha_{dt}$ given in equation~\ref{eqn:app_bases} in terms of $\{\varepsilon_L, \varepsilon_R, \varepsilon_{corr}\}$.
The maximum contrast seen in figure~\ref{fig:2}~(b), $C_\mathrm{max}=1/3$ after noise incident on the initial state $\ket{00}$ can be understood by directly inspecting the space of possible pure two qubit states reached through common only rotations. The general pure state after common rotations is the separable identical two-qubit states,
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
\ket{\Psi} &=\ket{\psi}\otimes\ket{\psi}\\
\end{split}
\end{equation}
with $\ket{\psi}=\cos\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right)\ket{0}+e^{i\phi}\sin\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right)\ket{1}$ for a single qubit general state.
Depolarising into the symmetric separable two-qubit states is equivalent to averaging over the full Bloch sphere,
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
    \bar{\rho}_\mathrm{sym}&=\frac{1}{4\pi}\int_0^\pi\int_0^{2\pi}\left(\sin(\theta)\ket{\Psi}\bra{\Psi}\right)\mathrm{d}\phi\mathrm{d}\theta\\
    &=\frac{1}{3}\ket{00}\bra{00}+\frac{1}{3}\ket{11}\bra{11}+\frac{1}{3}\ket{\Psi^+}\bra{\Psi^+}
    \end{split}
\end{equation}
where the third term is the Bell state $\ket{\Psi^+} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{01}+\ket{10})$, with the expected $\frac{1}{3}$ population.\\
Although here the measured outcome, $\Pi(\phi)$, suggests that multiple samples across $\phi$ are needed to fit a cosine and extract the contrast $C$, in fact, only a single RB experiment with fixed $\phi = 0$, executing a global rotation, can be used to recover $C$.\\

\subsection{State preparation errors}\label{app:spam}
Similar to previous work on subspace leakage benchmarking~\cite{sutherland_subspace_2025}, state preparation errors can lead to non-trivial modifications of the correlated population decay curves. 
First, we consider independent state preparation on $2$ qubits. We model independent errors as probabilistic bit flips. To first order in error, on a single qubit we have,
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
    \rho_{sp}^{(i)}&=(1-\varepsilon_{sp}^{(i)})\ket{0}\bra{0}+\varepsilon_{sp}^{(i)}\ket{1}\bra{1},\\
\end{split}
\end{equation}
where the superscript refers to which qubit, $L$, or $R$, the preparation error is relevant to.
This error may be included in Liouville form $\Lambda_{sp}^{(i)}$ to equation~\ref{eqn:rb_meas}, by acting on the initial state,
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{P}_{L,R}=\langle\langle L,R|\Lambda_T^m\Lambda_{sp}^{(L)}\Lambda_{sp}^{(R)}|00\rangle\rangle.
\end{equation}
In this picture, the state preparation errors scale only the overlap of the initial state with the sub-representations. 
The overlaps are multiplied by,
\begin{equation}
    A_{sp}^{(i)}=(1-\varepsilon_{sp}^{(i)}),
\end{equation}
if the qubit is involved non-trivially in the subspace projector, i.e. for the left and right qubit subsectors $A_{sp}=A_{sp}^{(i)}$.
Relevant to this work, the correlated survival populations with state preparation errors included are,
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{P}_{S,S} =  \frac{1}{4}+\frac{A_{sp}^{(L)}}{4}\alpha_L^m+\frac{A_{sp}^{(R)}}{4}\alpha_R^m + \frac{A_{sp}^{(L)}A_{sp}^{(R)}}{6}\alpha_{dt}^m+\frac{A_{sp}^{(L)}A_{sp}^{(R)}}{12}\alpha_{tr}^m.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Noise injection methods}\label{app:injection}

Deriving equation~\ref{eqn:detuning} used to fit detuning data. Applying detuning of $729$-$\unit{\nano\meter}$ laser $\delta$ from the carrier frequency. The propagator for a detuned carrier pulse is,
\begin{equation}
\hat U_\delta(t)= \exp\Bigl(\frac{-i}{2}t(\Omega\hat\sigma_x-\delta\hat\sigma_z)\Bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\Omega$ is the Rabi frequency.
For a $\pi/2$ pulse set duration, $\tau = \frac{\pi}{2\Omega}$,
\begin{equation}
\hat U_\delta(\tau)= \exp\Bigl(\frac{-i\pi}{4}(\hat\sigma_x-\eta\hat\sigma_z)\Bigr),
\end{equation}
where we have set $\eta=\delta/\Omega$.
Calculate fidelity of $\pi/2$ pulse with this detuning over Haar measure on Bloch sphere
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{F}_{avg}= \frac{|\mathrm{Tr}(U_{id}^\dagger U_{\delta})|^2+2}{6},
\end{equation}
where 
$\hat U_{id}(\tau)= \exp\Bigl(\frac{-i\pi}{4}\hat\sigma_x\Bigr)$,
Evaluating for the above expressions, and assuming $\eta<<1$, we find fidelity,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{F}_{avg}= 1 - \frac{\eta^2}{3} + O(\eta^4).
\end{equation}
The error per $R_\phi(\pi/2)$ gate is $\varepsilon = 1-\mathcal{F}_{avg}$. Assuming ideal twirling, the error of consecutive pulses should be proportional to sequence length. In the chosen decomposition of Clifford gates, there are on average $k=2.17$ $R(\pi/2)$ pulses (both virtual and physical).
The detuning offset is reparameterised as $\delta=\delta_0+\delta_L$, where $\delta_L$ is the setting of the laser detuning from the bare qubit frequency, and $\delta_0$ is the frequency offset when the qubit is dressed by the laser induced Stark shift. Finally, accounting for a correlated error source that does not originate from a frequency offset, $\varepsilon_0$, the desired expression for correlated error per Clifford is found,
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon_{corr} = \varepsilon_0 + k\Bigl(\frac{\delta_L - \delta_0}{\Omega}\Bigr)^2.
\end{equation}

``k-copy" RB may also be used to probe non-Markovian (time correlated) noise, which is of fundamental interest for QEC schemes~\cite{}. Repeating identical Clifford RB sequences at two distinct times on a single qubit and measuring correlated survival populations is analogous to parallel application of ``k-copy" RB on a pair of qubits. The analysis of the correlated populations is identical; however, common noise no longer discerns spatial correlations but rather indicates temporal correlations. A simple example of temporally correlated noise is shown for a single trapped Calcium ion with noise injected via random phase jitter of the $729$-$\unit{\nano\meter}$ laser. Identical Clifford RB sequences are executed one minute apart for three repeats. For the first two repeats, the random phase offsets are generated using the same random seed, and the final repeat, a different random seed is used. The time correlated populations can be seen in figure~\ref{fig:5} for both identical and non-identical errors in time. For identical errors, $\varepsilon_\mathrm{com}=,\varepsilon_L=,\varepsilon_R=$, while for non-identical errors, correlated errors are converted to locally independent: $\varepsilon_\mathrm{com}=,\varepsilon_L=,\varepsilon_R=$.\\
